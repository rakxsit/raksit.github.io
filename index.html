I am a Ph.D. candidate in the Linguistics Department at UC Berkeley. My research harnesses computational, corpus, and experimental methods to investigate variation within and across speakers in the usage of "primary" and "secondary" cues in the production and perception of a phonological contrast and the relationship between cue weighting, contrast, and sound change. My work also probes the conditions and factors that give rise to a situation that is ripe for change. I have explored these questions through laboratory methodology, corpus analysis, and neural net simulation. I work with Austroasiatic communities in Northeast Thailand (Kuy [ISO 639-3: kdt], Thro [ISO 639-3: sss], and So Thavung [ISO 639-3: thm]) and Southern Ryukyuan communities in Okinawa Prefecture (Yaeyama [ISO 639-3: rys], Dunan [ISO 639-3: yoi]). I also carry out documentation and revitalization work; I have been developing a website for the Meeramuni language with heritage speaker Madoka Hammine (半嶺まどか) and am currently documenting the structure and tools of the website for user-friendly and feasible replication for other languages.

My dissertation, co-advised by Susan Lin and Andrew Garrett, examines the interaction between multilingualism, transitioning social situations, and the shifting of cue weights in the Kuy register contrast in a quadrilingual community. The findings contribute to elucidating the specific mechanisms through which language contact and multilingualism may play a role in enhancing cues and catalyzing sound change.

Research

Multilingualism & Sound Change

Why is tone an areal feature often found across unrelated languages? I investigate this question in the context of Mainland Southeast Asia, exploring the hypothesis that bi-/multilingualism may trigger changes in cue weighting through a perception and production study on a voice quality contrast in Kuy [kdt] (Katuic, Austroasiatic) as spoken in Sisaket, Thailand. The results suggest that a speaker's usage of a tonal language can lead to heavier weighting of f0 cues in their usage of a non-tonal language. These changes may have been catalyzed by the recent rapid urbanization and centralization of Thailand. This case study provides contemporary evidence for multilingualism as a mechanism by which secondary differences may be enhanced, increasing the potential for trannsphonologization. Phonological Contrasts & Phonetic Cues

Phonological categories are signaled by a bundle of cues. Register, for example, utilizes not only voice quality, but also f0, intensity, and duration. I am interested in the extent to which the cues described as "primary" and "secondary" are encoded in phonological representation and how their distribution and usage inform us about directionality of sound change. In work with Sireemas Maspong and Francesco Burroni, we show through data in three typologically different languages we work with (Dunan, Pattani Malay, and Salentino) that even geminates, which are understood to use duration as the main cue, utilize a constellation of cues, including f0 and intensity, to a much greater extent than assumed. Files for this project may be found here. I have also looked at the question of cue ambiguity and enhancement through the lens of metathesis. In a project with Andrew Garrett, we looked at incipient metathesis in VCV sequences in Karuk [kyh] and its source in speaker-driven perceptual enhancement of a weakened cue. I have also explored the role of variability and magnitude of gestural overlap in sound change through a study using data from the Buckeye Corpus.

Language Documentation & Revitalization

I am involved with documentation, preservation, and revitalization efforts. I have been working on a website for learning Meeramuni Yaeyaman [rys] with heritage speaker Madoka Hammine (半嶺まどか), with the help of linguistics major Wendy Lau, and with voice recordings by Arakaki Shigeo (新垣重雄), Yamashiro Tamiko (山城たみ子), and Yamashiro Naokichi (山城直吉), and and artwork by linguistics majors Samantha Gies and Vivian Du. The teaching style employs inductive and associative learning, cultural relevance, and interactivity in order to engage users and enhance their learning experience by drawing on their pre-existing knowledge. If you would like a deeper explanation of the site, please contact me! Files for this project may be found here. I am also working on documentation to make features of the site replicable for other languages. In another project, I worked with Yamane Keiko (山根慶子) to translate the hit song Let It Go from Disney's Frozen into her language, Shikamuni (Ishigaki) Yaeyaman. The Shikamuni version, sung by a member of the Ishigaki community, may be found here. The Shikamuni version of a popular Japanese song by the Ishigaki band BEGIN may also be found here.

Modeling Morphological Change

How can computational modeling inform us about directionality in language change? To explore this question, I simulated changes in the nominal system from Latin to the descendant Romance languages using neural nets as a model of paradigm learning, evaluating the role of sound change and chance in morphological change. The results suggests that the system of Latin was in a state that was biased in the direction of syncretism. Files for this project may be found here.

Mystery Language! Introduction to Linguistic Analysis

In 2020, Edwin Ko, Wendy Liz Arbey López Márquez, and I introduced high schoolers to the basics of linguistic analysis by simulating a field methods class with Wendy's native language, Nuntajɨɨyi (Sierra Popoluca) [poi], a Mixe-Zoque language of Veracruz, Mexico. The students participated in elicitation of basic vocabulary and sentences to gain an appreciation for pattern-seeking and linguistic puzzles. This class was taught through Splash at Berkeley, whose goal is to recruit university students to teach high school students on a subject they are passionate about.

My name is pronounced ['ɹak.sɪt] or ['ɹʌk.sɪt] in English. In Thai (รักษ์สิทธิ์), it's pronounced pretty much how it's romanized, with some tone added in [ráksìt]. Lau (劉) is my father's last name, from Teochew [láo] / Cantonese [lɐ̀u], while Preechathammarach (ปรีชาธรรมรัช) [pri:.tɕa:.tʰam.ma.rát] is my mother's (you can pronounce it like "preecha-tama-rut"). My pronouns are he/him/his or they/them/their. My three sisters and I were all born and raised in New York City, but as kids, we went back to Thailand pretty often to see family. Like many Thai immigrants, our family is in the restaurant business. If you find yourself in New York, check out Lil Chef Mama, Thank You Come Again, and Thai Sliders if you're craving Thai food and Ramen Goku if you're craving Japanese! Like my family, I love cooking as well—you can check out some dishes I've made in the slideshow (still working on my plating and photography skills)!
